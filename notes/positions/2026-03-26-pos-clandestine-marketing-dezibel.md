## Thesis

Dezibel's ecosystem assets (oBitchuary Substack, Shit Eyes album, Hot Ghost incense) should launch as independent real-world products with no connection to the novel. The discovery that they're all part of the same fictional world becomes the viral moment — organic, earned, and self-amplifying. This clandestine approach, modeled on Neon's Longlegs campaign ($128M on sub-$10M P&A), is more effective than a traditional publicist-led press strategy because: (1) the medium matches the message (a novel delivered by text should be marketed through conversations, not press releases), (2) real products have a structural advantage over Longlegs' fake world (oBitchuary has real subscribers, not just a website), and (3) information asymmetry creates social currency that drives sharing.

**Kill test:** Send 5-7 beta readers Days 1-7 with zero context. Measure: do they share unprompted? Where? How? If organic sharing doesn't happen, revert to conventional press.

## Evidence For

- Structural advantage: dezibel's ecosystem products are real, not fabricated — stronger foundation than Longlegs' fake websites
- Information asymmetry creates social currency — withholding creates value, fragments become things people share to look in-the-know
- Medium matches message — a novel about text conversations marketed through conversations
- Longlegs precedent: $128M worldwide on sub-$10M P&A, zero TV ads, clandestine Reddit seeding
- The sentence "a Substack columnist, a Spotify album, and a Brooklyn incense brand turned out to be characters in a novel that texts you" travels on its own
- Guardian piece (Adrienne Matei, 1.3M weekly readers) is more powerful as explanation of existing conversation than as introduction

## Evidence Against

- **2026-03-26** (challenge) — Literary fiction readers are diffuse recommendation-sharers (Goodreads, book clubs, Substack), not organized puzzle-solvers like horror fans.
