## Context

Dezibel is a serialized novel delivered over iMessage. No existing category. The market isn't expecting its arrival — there's no shelf to put it on, no comp title to point to. The $100M target requires not just product-market fit but category creation. The question is how to de-risk launch when you can't rely on existing demand signals.

## Evidence So Far

- Serial (podcast) created a category in 2014 — fastest to 5M downloads, no precedent existed
- Wordle: zero marketing budget, emoji share grid was the viral mechanic, NYT acquired for $1-3M
- Calm: validated with "Do Nothing for Two Minutes" website before building app — cheapest possible test
- Bandersnatch: interactive format was its own marketing asset, 94% active participation
- Kindle Singles: had platform but no cultural identity — cautionary tale (format without community fails)
- Keitai shosetsu: 20M+ readers in Japan (2000s), 5 of top 10 bestsellers in 2007 — strongest historical precedent
- Cohort-based delivery: 70-96% completion vs 3-15% self-paced — structural scarcity beats artificial scarcity
- Slow media movement growing: Norwegian Slow TV (3M viewers), Tortoise Media (85k members at £80/year), Delayed Gratification (15 years, no ads)
- Gen Z appetite for digital detox: 63% planned social media detox (2024), 81% believe detoxes important
- The Cheaper Show proved Graeme can create formats that draw audiences at scale (12,500 at finale)
- Demo exists (Day 1 functional) but untested with real readers
- [[kill-test-full-project]] — A5 identifies specific behavioral gap: literary fiction readers don't decode-and-share like horror fans, meaning clandestine campaign has no proven audience behavior mechanic (2026-03-26, via /digest)
- Adrienne Matei (Guardian journalist, 1M+ readers/week) committed to write on traction
- Web Summit Vancouver May 11-14, 2026 — high-priority showcase opportunity (local, tech audience)
- 3-day SMS demo identified as cheapest validation test (see [[dezibel-marketing-validation]])
