## What You're Selling

Dezibel becomes a company (lawyer incorporates). The company owns the operational rights to everything: book delivery, app, merch, ecosystem products, sequel exploitation. Investors buy pieces of the company, not pieces of the book.

**Critical distinction:** IP (the manuscript, characters, world) should stay with Graeme personally, licensed to the company. Standard for creator-led ventures. Protects the work if the company fails.

## Three Investment Structures

### Option A: Equity (direct ownership)

- Investor gives $100K → gets ~2.7% of the company (at $3M pre-money)
- Permanent ownership
- Entitled to proportional share of profits and exit proceeds
- If Dezibel IP sells to Netflix for $10M, they get 2.7% = $270K
- **Simple. Permanent. They're partners.**
- Requires formal valuation negotiation now

### Option B: SAFE Note (recommended for Dezibel's stage)

SAFE = Simple Agreement for Future Equity

- Investor gives $100K now
- No percentage assigned today
- Promise: their $100K converts to equity later — at next priced round or exit — at a discount or valuation cap
- Example: SAFE at $3M cap. If next round values company at $6M, their $100K buys shares at the $3M price = 2× the shares a new investor gets
- **Delays valuation negotiation. Simplest paperwork. Standard for pre-revenue.**
- Y Combinator created the template — widely understood by angel investors
- Ryan Holmes and Tippett's networks do these routinely

### Option C: Revenue Share (no equity given away)

- Investor gives $100K
- Graeme pays back X% of revenue until investor receives 2–3× investment ($200–300K)
- Then payments stop — no ongoing obligation
- Investor never owns any part of the company or IP
- **Good for creative projects where creator wants full ownership.**
- Con: cash flowing out during growth phase when it's needed most
- Con: if revenue is slow, repayment drags

## IP Protection Structure (Suggested)

```
Graeme Berglund (personally)
    │
    │ owns: manuscript, characters, world, sequel rights,
    │       music rights (shit_eyes), all creative IP
    │
    ├── LICENSES IP TO ──→ Dezibel Inc. (the company)
    │                        │
    │                        ├── Operates: delivery platform, app, merch store
    │                        ├── Holds: vendor contracts, employee agreements
    │                        ├── Owns: brand assets, platform code, subscriber data
    │                        └── Investors own equity HERE (not in the IP)
    │
    └── If company fails → IP returns to Graeme, free and clear
```

**Non-negotiable:** The manuscript and creative IP stay with Graeme. The company licenses exploitation rights. If things go sideways, Graeme walks away with his book. This is standard for creator-led ventures (film, music, publishing all work this way).

1. **Structure:** SAFE notes at $3M valuation cap
2. **Investor count:** 5–8 investors at $75K–$150K each
3. **Total raise:** $700K (with $125K contingency built into budget)
4. **IP:** Stays with Graeme personally, licensed to company
5. **Legal cost to set up:** $25–35K (company incorporation, SAFE template, IP license agreement, investor agreements)

## What Investors Get

At $3M pre-money, $700K raise:
- ~19% of the company collectively
- Rights to proportional share of: subscription revenue, merch revenue, licensing deals, film/adaptation deals (via company)
- No direct IP ownership — their value is in the company's license to exploit the IP
- Standard information rights (quarterly updates)
- No board seat unless negotiated (avoid giving board seats to angels — complicates decisions)

## What Graeme Keeps

- 81% of the company
- 100% of the creative IP (manuscript, characters, world, sequel)
- Full creative control (non-negotiable in investor agreements)
- Right to make all story, casting, and brand decisions
- License can be structured with creative control provisions

## Comparable Structures

- **Substack advances:** Authors retain IP, platform gets distribution rights. Similar to Dezibel licensing model.
- **Indie film financing:** Director/writer retains screenplay rights, production company has exploitation license. Investors buy into production company. Exactly this structure.
- **Music:** Artist retains masters (when negotiated well), label has distribution license. Revenue flows through the license.

3. How to structure the IP license? (Exclusive vs. non-exclusive, term, territory)
4. What happens to the license if the company is sold? (IP should revert or transfer with Graeme's consent)
