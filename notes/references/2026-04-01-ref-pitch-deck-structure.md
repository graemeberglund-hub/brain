# Dezibel Pitch Deck — Recommended Structure

Research compiled 2026-04-01 for investor meetings week of April 7. Findings synthesized from YC guidance, angel investor pitch research, sextech fundraising patterns, and pre-revenue SAFE best practices — then filtered for what actually applies to dezibel (cultural product, solo founder, erotic content, friends-and-family/angel audience).

---

## Meta-decisions first

### How many slides?

10-12. YC recommends 10-12 for seed. Angel pitches are 10-15 minutes of presentation plus Q&A. For informal meetings (coffee, dinner), you may not even project slides — the deck becomes a leave-behind. Build it so it works both ways: presentable AND forwardable without narration.

### How long is the pitch?

10-15 minutes of talking, max. For informal angel/F&F meetings, plan for 20-30 minutes including interruptions, with 15-20 minutes of Q&A after. The more time in discussion, the better — you learn what matters to each investor. Do not fill the hour with slides.

### What format?

Visually simple. YC's explicit advice: "Visually stunning slides often hurt seed-stage pitches." Large text, clear points. BUT — this is a creative product. The deck should feel like the product: beautiful, confident, restrained. Not a SaaS template. Think art book layout, not McKinsey. The deck IS a taste test for the product's aesthetic.

### What's different about pitching a cultural product?

Standard SaaS decks lead with problem → solution → metrics. Cultural products lead with vision → proof of craft → market opportunity. The emotional sequence is: **make them feel something → show them it's real → show them it's a business.** Not the reverse.

The hero of a SaaS pitch is the customer with a problem. The hero of a cultural product pitch is the vision — and the founder's ability to execute it. At this stage, at this round size, investors are betting on Graeme, not on a TAM slide.

## Recommended slide structure: 12 slides

### Slide 1 — TITLE / HOOK

**What goes on it:** Title treatment. One-sentence descriptor. No logo soup, no team photo.

**Text:** "DEZIBEL — A novel that texts you back."

**Emotional job:** Intrigue. The investor should lean forward. This sentence does the work of a problem slide and a solution slide simultaneously — it implies both the gap (novels don't do this) and the product (this one does).

**Vault data:** Tagline from marketing strategy. One-sentence positioning: "The first literary novel delivered by iMessage — a love story about two writers, a seven-year silence, and an erotic co-writing project, received on your phone like a text from someone you've been waiting to hear from."

**Design note:** This slide should look like nothing else they've seen in a pitch deck. It should look like a book cover or a film poster. Set the tone immediately.

---

### Slide 2 — THE EXPERIENCE (not "the product")

**What goes on it:** What the reader actually experiences. Walk through it in 4-5 lines, present tense. Show the phone. Show a sample message. Make them feel the format.

**Text (example framing):**
- You subscribe. The following Sunday at 12:08pm, a text arrives from an unknown number.
- Over 42 days, a story unfolds — two writers reconnecting after seven years of silence.
- The messages arrive like real texts. You read them on your phone, in your life, in real time.
- Video pieces arrive — the actors playing these characters, on film. This is a multimedia literary experience: text, video, audio, haptics.
- On Day 7, you're offered a wearable device that syncs to the erotic scenes.
- On Day 36, something happens that changes everything.

**Emotional job:** Desire. The investor should want to experience this. Not understand it — want it. This is the "make them feel something" slide.

**Vault data:** 42-day structure, Sunday-to-Sunday delivery, haptic opt-in at Day 7, Emma's death at Day 36.

**Design note:** Show an actual phone screen with a message thread. If the demo exists, screenshot it. This slide is the product.

---

### Slide 3 — WHY NOW

**What goes on it:** Cultural moment + market timing. Three forces converging.

**Key points:**
1. **Attention fatigue is a health crisis.** 63% of Gen Z planned a social media detox in 2024. Slow media is a movement: Norwegian Slow TV (3M viewers), Tortoise Media (85K members at £80/year). People are paying for constraint.
2. **Audio erotica proved the market.** Quinn: $12M+ ARR, 440% YoY growth, 75%+ women users. Dipsea: acquired at $6M ARR. Women will pay for intimate content framed as wellness, not porn.
3. **No one has combined literary fiction with phone-native delivery.** Keitai shosetsu hit 20M readers in Japan's feature phone era. The format works. It just hasn't been done well, in English, with production value.

**Emotional job:** Inevitability. This isn't a weird idea — it's an obvious idea whose time has come. The only question is who does it first.

**Vault data:** Gen Z detox stats, Slow TV/Tortoise, Quinn ARR ($12M+), Dipsea acquisition, keitai shosetsu, market-entry-strategy position.

---

### Slide 4 — MARKET OPPORTUNITY

**What goes on it:** TAM/SAM/SOM — but honest. Not the standard three concentric circles. Frame it as: here's the market that exists, here's the sliver we're taking, here's why that sliver is enough.

**Key numbers:**
- Global sex-tech market: $50B (2025), growing 16-18% CAGR
- Connected/smart device segment: 38-44% of that = ~$19-22B
- Audio erotica (Quinn + Dipsea precedent): $18M+ ARR combined from two startups
- Dezibel's addressable: literary fiction readers (28-45, women-skewing) who would pay for a premium intimate experience. SAM: $1.6B. Target Year 1: $500K-$2M (10,000-40,000 subscribers at $49-159).

**Emotional job:** Credibility. Not "this is a trillion-dollar market" — but "this market is real, growing, and under-served by anything with production value."

**Vault data:** Haptic sex-tech market research (full reference note), Quinn/Dipsea numbers, pricing model position.

**Design note:** Lead with Quinn's numbers. A real company doing $12M ARR in audio erotica is worth more than any TAM circle.

---

### Slide 5 — COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE

**What goes on it:** 2x2 matrix or simple positioning chart. NOT a feature comparison table.

**Axes suggestion:** Production value (low → high) vs. Delivery intimacy (passive → phone-native).

**Positioning:**
- Quinn / Dipsea: Medium production, passive delivery (you go to an app, press play)
- Kindle Vella / Radish / serialized fiction apps: Low production, app-native but not intimate
- Immersive theatre (Sleep No More, Punchdrunk): High production, in-person only
- DEZIBEL: High production, phone-native delivery. The only premium single-title experience delivered through the most intimate screen you own.

**Emotional job:** Category clarity. Dezibel isn't competing with any of these directly. It's creating a new quadrant. The competitive slide's job is to show the white space, not list competitors.

**Key line:** "Every library model in this space has died. The market is waiting for a single premium title done right."

**Vault data:** Kill test on format novelty ("genuinely new, not just new for publishing"), aspirational positioning position, market entry strategy evidence.

---

### Slide 6 — TRACTION / PROOF OF CRAFT

**What goes on it:** This is the most important slide for a pre-revenue creative product. You don't have revenue. You have something better for this audience: proof that the thing exists and is good.

**Key proof points:**
- 70% written (243 pages, 7,719 lines). This is not an idea — it's a manuscript.
- Functional demo exists. Day 1 works. You can show it on your phone right now.
- Cast attached and performing on film: Jodi Balfour (Ted Lasso) as Emma, Edo Van Breemen (Brasstronaut) as Hasta — not just voices, but playing their characters in video content delivered within the experience. Abbi Jacobson (Broad City / A League of Their Own) as Jane (photos + audio).
- Production team confirmed: Sound design (Eugenio), brand + web (Post Projects / Alex Nelson), trailer director (Kiddo Films).
- Adrienne Matei (Guardian, 1.3M weekly readers) committed to write the piece on traction.
- 12,500 attendance at The Cheaper Show finale — proof Graeme can build audiences for novel formats.

**Emotional job:** Confidence. This founder executes. The hardest part (writing the thing) is 70% done. The team is real. The press is lined up. This is not a pitch for an idea — it's a pitch for the final push.

**Vault data:** Story status from deep read, cast list, production team, Guardian commitment, Cheaper Show history.

**Design note:** If any slide gets an image, it's this one. A photo of Jodi Balfour. A screenshot of the demo. A shot from The Cheaper Show with 12,500 people.

**CRITICAL:** Do not include a traction slide with zero metrics and try to spin it. "70% written + cast attached + demo functional" IS the traction. Own it directly.

---

### Slide 7 — BUSINESS MODEL

**What goes on it:** Revenue streams, unit economics, and the expansion path. Keep it to one slide.

**Revenue tiers:**
- Standard experience: $49 (42-day story via SMS/iMessage)
- Haptic Edition: $149-159 (story + Lovense Ferri wearable device)
- oBitchuary Substack: $3/month (companion product, runs independently)
- Physical ecosystem: Hot Ghost incense ($18-28), Pyrrha charm ($85-150), t-shirts ($70)
- Reread: 30% discount on subsequent cohorts

**Unit economics (standard):** ~90%+ margin. Digital delivery, near-zero COGS.

**Unit economics (haptic bundle):** 8-44% margin depending on wholesale pricing (UNVERIFIED — see vault research). Critical variable is Lovense wholesale cost.

**Expansion path (post-proof):** Sequel (That Night — film, via Oddfellows), format licensing to other authors/publishers, international markets, paperback with exclusive ending.

**Emotional job:** This is a real business, not a passion project. Multiple revenue streams. High margins on the core product. And a clear path from $49 subscriptions to a much larger IP play.

**Vault data:** Pricing model position, haptic market research margin breakdown, ecosystem product details from deep read.

---

### Slide 8 — GO-TO-MARKET

**What goes on it:** How you get to 500 Day 1 subscribers and 2,000 by Day 14. Not a vague "marketing plan" — a specific sequence.

**The funnel:**
1. **Ecosystem seeding (T-8 weeks):** oBitchuary goes live, Shit Eyes singles release, Hot Ghost in Brooklyn stores. None announce dezibel. Connection is the discovery.
2. **Beta readers (T-4 weeks):** 25 people receive Day 1 with no context. Kill test: do they share organically?
3. **Free Day 1 preview (T-2 weeks):** Landing page. Free preview captures email. Nurture sequence converts at 15-25% vs. 2-3% for cold $49 purchase.
4. **Press (T-1 week):** Guardian piece (Adrienne Matei, 1.3M weekly). Embargo lifts Day 3-5.
5. **Day 1:** Text arrives at 12:08pm PST. No announcement.
6. **Day 7:** Haptic integration becomes public. Second press wave.
7. **Day 36:** Emma dies. Biggest organic moment.

**Emotional job:** Sophistication. This isn't "we'll figure out marketing later." The launch sequence is designed. The unconventional campaign (Longlegs playbook) shows strategic creativity — the same mind that wrote the novel is running the go-to-market.

**Vault data:** Marketing strategy from deep read, funnel architecture position (free preview converts 15-25% vs. 2-3% direct), Guardian funnel math, unconventional campaign details.

---

### Slide 9 — TEAM + ADVISORS

**What goes on it:** Graeme front and center. Then the extended team. Then advisors. Frame this as a production — credits, not org chart.

**How to handle "solo founder" concern:**
1. Don't hide it. Name it: "I wrote it, I'm directing the trailer, I'm running the launch. This is an author-led project, not a startup looking for a CTO."
2. Show the production team: sound design, brand, web, trailer — all confirmed, all named.
3. Show the advisors: Ryan Holmes (tech), Michael Tippett (business, multiple exits).
4. Name the key hire this round enables: operations producer (Millardo or Elliat, both candidates identified).
5. Show the cast as part of the team — Jodi Balfour and Abbi Jacobson lend credibility that no CTO slide ever could.

**Emotional job:** Trust. At F&F/angel stage, they're investing in Graeme. The team slide's job is to show that Graeme has the judgment to surround himself with the right people — and that those people have already said yes.

**Vault data:** Full people list from deep read, advisor contacts, producer candidates.

**Key framing:** "This is structured like a film production, not a startup. The writer-director model works in film. It works here."

---

### Slide 10 — THE ASK

**What goes on it:** How much, what structure, what it funds, what milestones it reaches.

**The ask:**
- Raising $700-850K on a post-money SAFE
- Valuation cap: $7-10M (typical for pre-revenue SAFE at this round size per 2025/2026 benchmarks; median cap for rounds $250K-$1M is $10M)
- Staged option: $500K initial close → additional $150-350K post first-cohort data

**Use of funds (high-level, not line items):**
- Finish the product (editing, extraction, cast recording): ~30%
- Build the platform (app, delivery at scale, tech support): ~25%
- Launch (brand, trailer, marketing activation): ~25%
- Operations (legal, producer, living expenses, contingency): ~20%

**Milestones this capital reaches:**
1. Product complete and production-ready
2. Platform built and tested at scale
3. First cohort launched (500+ subscribers Day 1 target)
4. Revenue data from first 42 days — the evidence for a larger raise

**Emotional job:** Clarity and confidence. Not desperation. "This is what I need, this is what it buys, this is what you'll see." The staged option shows financial sophistication — it tells investors they can start smaller and add after proof.

**Vault data:** Budget from deep read ($571K deck figure, $745-870K realistic), raise strategy position (staged vs. single), SAFE benchmarks from research.

**Do NOT include:** 5-year financial projections. Research is unanimous: at pre-seed, 5-year projections signal naivety. Show milestones instead.

---

### Slide 11 — VISION / WHERE THIS GOES

**What goes on it:** The $100M picture. Not as a projection — as a vision. This is where you earn the right to dream big, because you've already shown the product is real.

**Key beats:**
- Dezibel is Title 1. The format is the platform.
- Sequel: "That Night" — a film. Oddfellows Pictures ($200M+ global sales) are close friends and potential producers.
- Format licensing: other authors publish through the dezibel delivery system. Dezibel becomes a label, not just a book.
- International: The format is language-agnostic. SMS works everywhere.
- Adjacent industries: A24, Neon — existing working relationships. The IP has film, TV, and adaptation value.

**Emotional job:** Ambition. After 10 slides of proof and pragmatism, this is the moment to go big. "You're not investing in a book. You're investing in the first title of a new medium."

**Vault data:** $100M goal position, Oddfellows reference, A24/Neon relationships from operator state, format licensing concept.

---

### Slide 12 — CLOSING

**What goes on it:** Contact info. One image. One line.

**Text:** "A novel that texts you back. Let me show you Day 1."

**Emotional job:** The offer to demo. Every pitch should end with a demonstration, not a slide. If you're in person, hand them your phone. Let them read the first few messages. The product sells itself.

## How to handle specific concerns

### The erotic content angle

**Do not lead with it. Do not hide it.** The research is clear: founders who frame sexual content as "wellness" get funded; founders who lead with "sex" don't. But dezibel isn't a sex product — it's a literary product with erotic scenes.

**Recommended framing:** "The story includes erotic content — it's literary fiction about desire and intimacy, not pornography. The haptic integration is an opt-in enhancement, not the core product. Quinn proved the market: $12M ARR, 75% women, positioned as sexual wellness. We're positioned as literary fiction with an intimate delivery format."

**When to address it:** Slide 2 (the experience) mentions it naturally. Slide 4 (market) uses Quinn as the comp. Don't dedicate a slide to it. If investors ask, answer directly and move on.

**Language to use:** "intimate content," "erotic scenes," "sexual wellness." Not: "porn," "adult content," "NSFW."

### The solo founder question

**Own it as a feature, not a bug.** Research says: at the earliest stage, investors are betting on the founder, not the org chart. And this is an author-led project — the solo-creator model is the norm in publishing and film, not a red flag.

**If asked:** "This is a writer-director project. I wrote it, I'll direct the trailer, I'm designing the launch. That's not a gap — that's the point. The operations producer is the first hire post-raise, and I have two candidates."

### Financial projections

**Do not include 5-year projections.** Every source — YC, angel investors, pitch deck analysts — says the same thing: pre-revenue projections are fiction, and sophisticated investors know it. Including them signals you don't know your audience.

**What to show instead:**
- Unit economics at $49 and $149 (margins)
- Break-even subscriber count (~10,000 on $500K raise, ~15,000 on $725K)
- Quinn as revenue comp ($12M ARR on $3.2M raised — 3.75x return on capital in ARR alone)

### Canadian founder, US market

Not a concern for angels/F&F. For later institutional rounds, the standard answer: incorporate in Delaware, bank in the US, operate wherever you want. For this round, it's a non-issue.

### The "concert model" (one-time purchase, not subscription)

**Pitch it as a feature:** "Every library model in serialized fiction has died — Kindle Vella, Radish, Hooked. Subscriptions commoditize stories. Dezibel is an experience you buy once, like a concert ticket or escape room. $49 for 42 days. The scarcity is real — cohorts start on Sundays, the story ends. You were there or you weren't."

**The expansion answer:** "Recurring revenue comes from new titles, new cohorts, the companion Substack, and ecosystem products. The core product is one-time, but the platform generates repeat revenue across titles."

## Reference decks to study

These are not direct comps — dezibel is genuinely novel — but each solved a version of the same problem (pitching a cultural/experiential product to investors):

1. **MasterClass (Series A, 2015):** Pitched celebrity-taught online classes. Pre-revenue. Led with the talent (Dustin Hoffman, Serena Williams) and the production value. Raised on vision + proof of craft.
2. **Calm (seed, 2012):** Validated with "Do Nothing for Two Minutes" — a one-page website. Pitched the cultural moment (mindfulness), not the app. Simplest possible proof before asking for money.
3. **Quinn (seed, $3.2M, 2022):** Audio erotica. Caroline Spiegel framed it as sexual wellness, not porn. Raised from a mix of angels including Adrian Grenier. "If I had gone in and simply pitched a sex angle then I don't think we would have got the funding."
4. **Airbnb (seed, 2008):** The most famous deck ever. 10 slides. Problem → solution → market → product. But the magic was showing the actual product screenshots. The product sold itself.
5. **Legion M (2022, $11M):** Fan-owned entertainment company. Proved that media/entertainment raises work when investors feel like participants, not just check-writers.

## Pre-meeting checklist

- [ ] Demo on your phone — charged, loaded, ready to hand over
- [ ] Deck exported as PDF for forwarding after the meeting
- [ ] Know the SAFE terms cold: valuation cap, discount (if any), pro-rata rights
- [ ] Prepare 2-minute verbal pitch (no slides) for informal settings
- [ ] Have the Quinn numbers memorized ($12M ARR, $3.2M raised, 75% women, 440% YoY)
- [ ] Have the budget breakdown ready if asked (not in the deck, but in your head)
- [ ] Know your "what if this doesn't work" answer: "The novel gets published traditionally. The format research has value. The IP exists regardless."

---

## UNVERIFIED items that affect the pitch

1. **$49 price point:** No A/B test, no WTP survey, no precedent for SMS fiction pricing. If an investor pushes on price validation, acknowledge it directly: "Price is untested. First cohort is the test."
2. **Lovense wholesale pricing:** Haptic bundle margins depend entirely on wholesale cost. Range is 8-44%. If presenting the haptic bundle, say "margins depend on wholesale terms we're negotiating" not "44% margins."
3. **500 Day 1 subscriber target:** Funnel modeling shows 88 (base) to 377 (bull) with current strategy. Free preview funnel could push this to 2,900+, but that architecture isn't built yet. Don't promise 500 in the deck.
4. **Guardian piece timing:** The entire Day 1 funnel depends on the Guardian piece running pre-launch or Day 3-5. Without it, Day 1 drops from ~535 to ~145. Adrienne is committed but timing is not locked.
