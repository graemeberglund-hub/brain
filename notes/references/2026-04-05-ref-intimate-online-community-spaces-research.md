# Intimate Online Community Spaces Research

**Date:** 2026-04-05
**Purpose:** Deep survey of beautiful, artful, intimate online community platforms — the opposite of cold BBS/forum systems. Specific examples with UX descriptions, organized by category. Informing Dezibel community space design.

---

## 1. Art-Forward Community Platforms

### Are.na
**URL:** https://www.are.na
**What it is:** A visual research and mood-boarding platform that doubles as a slow-conversation community. Founded 2011. Member-supported, no ads, no algorithm.

**What it looks like:** Minimal, almost brutalist. Clean white backgrounds, a monospaced-adjacent sans-serif font (their custom "areal"), generous whitespace. The color system is restrained: soft greens for public channels, reds for private, grays for closed. No hero images, no marketing gloss. It looks like a tool designed by someone who reads Dieter Rams before breakfast.

**How it feels:** Quiet. Deliberately slow. You collect blocks (images, links, text, files) into channels, and channels can be connected to other channels. The feed is chronological and personal — no algorithmic amplification. The community emerges from people connecting their channels to yours. You discover people by stumbling into their collections, not through follower counts. It feels like browsing someone's studio desk rather than their social media profile.

**What makes it warm:** The absence of metrics. No likes, no follower counts visible by default. The act of connecting someone's block to your channel is the primary social gesture — it says "I see this the way you do" without performing it publicly. The Explore page shows spaces being actively edited, creating a sense of liveness without urgency.

**Small groups:** Channels can be collaborative (invite-only or open). Many small creative groups use shared channels as ongoing research spaces. The conversation happens through curation rather than comments.

**Dezibel relevance:** HIGH. The channel-as-conversation model — where the discussion prompt is the collected material, not a text post — maps directly to a literary community where the book itself is the shared object. Are.na proves you can build community through curation alone.

---

### Cosmos
**URL:** https://www.cosmos.so
**What it is:** A visual curation platform for creatives (designers, architects, stylists, art directors). Launched 2021 by Andy McCune (previously sold Unfold to Squarespace). Positioned as "Pinterest for people with taste."

**What it looks like:** Sleek, dark-mode-first UI. Content gets saved as "Elements" organized into "Clusters" — mood-board-style collections. AI auto-tags saved items by color, mood, and style so you can search by palette or visual similarity. No traditional feed — you browse collections.

**How it feels:** Like flipping through someone's carefully curated lookbook. The absence of public likes removes the competitive anxiety of Instagram. No comments section (or minimal). The platform is about collecting and organizing visual inspiration, not broadcasting. In March 2026, Cosmos tapped actress Odessa A'zion to star in a short film celebrating the platform — they position themselves as a cultural project, not just an app.

**What makes it warm vs cold:** Warm because it strips engagement metrics entirely. Cold because it's primarily solo — you curate for yourself. The community layer is thinner than Are.na's. It's more "beautiful tool" than "gathering place."

**Small groups:** Limited. It's primarily a personal curation tool. Collaboration features are nascent.

**Dezibel relevance:** MEDIUM. The design language is instructive — proves that ad-free, metric-free platforms can attract serious creative users. The AI tagging by mood/color is interesting for a literary context (imagine tagging passages by emotional tone). But the community layer is too thin for Dezibel's needs.

---

### Kinopio
**URL:** https://kinopio.club
**What it is:** A spatial thinking canvas where you place cards (text, images, links) on an infinite board and draw connections between them. Built by Pirijan Ketheswaran (formerly Are.na's designer). Community-funded.

**What it looks like:** Playful, handmade. Cards sit on a dotted-grid canvas. You drag them around freely, draw lines between them, color them. The aesthetic is somewhere between a whiteboard, a corkboard, and a child's art project. Rounded corners, soft colors, a hand-drawn quality to the UI chrome. No rigid columns or rows — everything floats.

**How it feels:** Intimate and personal. Because cards are placed spatially rather than listed linearly, your thinking becomes visible as a landscape. The redesign uses a functional color system: green for streaks, blue for metadata, pink for sharing, purple for composing. The "Explore" page shows community spaces being actively edited — you can watch someone think in real time. "Live Spaces" shows public boards currently being worked on.

**What makes it warm:** The spatial metaphor itself. Placing an idea next to another idea, drawing a line between them — these are gestures that feel handmade, not algorithmic. Comments can be left on any card. The whole thing feels like passing notes in class, or annotating a friend's sketchbook.

**Small groups:** Yes. Spaces can be private or public, invite-only or open. Groups can manage shared spaces. The collaboration is real-time — you see other people's cursors.

**Dezibel relevance:** HIGH. The spatial thinking model is powerful for a literary community. Imagine a shared space where readers place their reactions to a chapter as cards on a canvas, drawing connections between moments. It transforms discussion from linear thread to visual map. Also: Kinopio was built by an Are.na alum, so the DNA of "curation as conversation" runs deep.

---

### Futureland
**URL:** https://futureland.tv
**What it is:** A creative journaling platform with community features. Track habits, projects, and creative practice through daily posts to themed "journals." Built around streaks and accountability.

**What it looks like:** Minimal, functional. Journals you post to daily appear as "Today" journals with visible streak counts. Everything else is "Not Today." The redesign uses a distinctive color system: green for streaks, blue for metadata, pink for sharing, purple for composing. Posts tend to be short breezy updates or longer reflections.

**How it feels:** Like a creative practice community rather than a social network. The emphasis on streaks creates gentle accountability without competition. You follow people and see their journal updates — their daily practice of making, learning, building. It's voyeuristic in the best way: watching someone show up every day to their craft.

**What makes it warm:** The tight-knit community. The platform supports both public and private posts within shared spaces. Onboarding includes a welcome journal with in-depth explanations. Streak history preserves records of past streaks so losing one doesn't feel devastating. Customizable posting schedules forgive missed days.

**Small groups:** Yes. Custom groups for organizing journals. Challenges for shared accountability. Close friends can follow each other's progress.

**Dezibel relevance:** MEDIUM-HIGH. The journaling-as-community model is interesting for Dezibel. Imagine subscribers keeping a reading journal that's visible to their cohort — daily reflections on what they read, how it hit. Streak mechanics could track "days engaged" without being punitive. The accountability-without-competition balance is exactly right.

## 2. Spatial/Visual Chat Experiments

### Gather.town
**URL:** https://www.gather.town
**What it is:** A 2D video-chat platform where your avatar walks around a pixelated map. Spatial audio means you hear people near you and can't hear people far away. Founded 2020.

**What it looks like:** Retro pixel art — looks like a top-down SNES RPG. Your avatar is a tiny sprite. You walk through customizable rooms: offices, lounges, gardens, conference halls. Other people appear as their own sprites with small video bubbles above their heads when you're close enough.

**How it feels:** Surprisingly warm once you acclimate to the aesthetic. The spatial audio is the key innovation — walking up to someone and having their voice fade in creates a sense of physical proximity that Zoom entirely lacks. You can "bump into" people. You can drift away from a boring conversation by literally walking away. Side conversations happen naturally.

**What makes it warm vs cold:** Warm because of serendipity — you encounter people rather than scheduling time with them. Cold because the pixel aesthetic is inherently playful/casual, which may not suit serious creative discussion. The gamification (customizable spaces, interactive objects) can feel more "corporate team-building" than "intimate salon."

**Small groups:** Excellent for small groups. Private rooms, breakout spaces, quiet corners. The spatial metaphor naturally creates pods of 3-5 people within larger gatherings.

**Dezibel relevance:** LOW-MEDIUM. The spatial audio concept is fascinating but the execution is too gamified for Dezibel's tone. However, the core insight — that proximity creates intimacy, and the ability to drift between conversations creates serendipity — is deeply relevant.

---

### Figma Multiplayer (Cursors as Presence)
**URL:** https://www.figma.com
**What it is:** A collaborative design tool where multiple people edit simultaneously. Not a community platform, but its multiplayer features have become a template for "presence" in digital spaces.

**What it looks like:** Each collaborator appears as a named, color-coded cursor floating on the shared canvas. You see them move, select, type, and create in real time. In FigJam (the whiteboard product), avatars can chat, emote, stamp, and even high-five.

**How it feels:** Like being in the same room. The cursor is a surprisingly powerful presence indicator — seeing someone's cursor pause, then move decisively, communicates thought in a way that text chat cannot. Figma has invested in the emotional dimension: skeuomorphic cursors that reference physical materiality, customizable avatars, emoji reactions. On April Fools', they shipped old-school cursor designs that users loved enough to petition for permanently.

**What makes it warm:** The cursor as body language. A cursor hovering near your work says "I'm looking at what you made." A cursor waving says "I see you." There's no visual metaphor for personal expression quite like a cursor blinking in a shared document.

**Caveats:** Some users report anxiety from multiplayer mode — the feeling of being watched while working. Presence can become surveillance. Important design lesson: presence should be opt-in and adjustable.

**Dezibel relevance:** MEDIUM. The cursor-as-presence concept could translate to a reading experience — imagine seeing that 3 other people in your cohort are currently reading the same chapter, their "position" visible as soft dots on a progress bar. Not tracking them, just knowing they're there. Presence without surveillance.

---

### New Art City
**URL:** https://newart.city
**What it is:** A virtual exhibition platform for new media art. Multiplayer 3D browser environments where visitors walk through curated shows together. Founded by Don Hanson and Sammie Veeler.

**What it looks like:** Each exhibition is a unique 3D environment — some look like galleries, some like alien landscapes, some like abstract architectural spaces. Visitors appear as simple avatars moving through the space. No login required. Built-in group chat and guestbook. A no-code editor lets curators build 3D exhibition spaces without modeling experience.

**How it feels:** Like visiting a gallery with strangers. The copresence — seeing other visitors move through the same space, stopping at the same works — creates a shared experience that static web galleries completely lack. The chat layer adds a whispered-conversation quality, like murmuring to the person next to you in a museum. The guestbook lets visitors leave screenshots and comments that persist.

**What makes it warm:** The emphasis on "copresence" — the simultaneous experience of being somewhere together. Rather than solitary browsing, you're walking through curated spaces alongside others. The no-registration requirement lowers the barrier to casual visits. The guestbook creates a trace of shared experience.

**Small groups:** Natural. Spatial audio means you can walk away from the crowd for a private conversation within the exhibition space.

**Dezibel relevance:** HIGH. New Art City is the closest existing model to what a Dezibel community space could feel like: a curated environment where people experience something together, with chat and presence layered on top of the primary experience (art in their case, story in ours). The guestbook concept — persistent visitor traces — is powerful for serialized fiction.

---

### Mozilla Hubs
**URL:** https://hubs.mozilla.com
**What it is:** A social 3D platform for creating private virtual rooms in your browser. Open source. Used extensively for art exhibitions during COVID.

**What it looks like:** Customizable 3D environments — conference rooms, galleries, landscapes. Avatars are simple but expressive. Spatial audio: voices get louder as avatars approach and fade as they walk away (up to ~10 virtual meters for private conversation without disturbing others).

**How it feels:** More functional than magical, but the spatial audio creates genuine moments of intimacy. Walking up to someone's avatar and hearing their voice emerge from silence is surprisingly emotional. Used by Ars Electronica, RIBI (Royal Institute of British Architects), AIGA, and numerous art schools for exhibitions and events during the pandemic.

**What makes it warm:** Spatial audio + customizable spaces + low barrier (browser-only, no install). The ability to walk away from a group for a quiet 1:1 conversation mirrors real-world social dynamics.

**Dezibel relevance:** LOW-MEDIUM. The technology is interesting but the 3D overhead may be unnecessary for Dezibel. The spatial audio principle is the extractable insight.

---

### Miro (as Discussion Space)
**URL:** https://miro.com
**What it is:** A collaborative infinite-canvas whiteboard. Not designed as a community platform, but widely repurposed for creative workshops, visual facilitation, and discussion.

**What it looks like:** Infinite white canvas with sticky notes, drawings, frameworks, images, and connectors. Multiple cursors visible. 2500+ community templates for workshops, brainstorms, retrospectives.

**How it feels:** In workshop contexts, Miro feels like a shared table covered in Post-its. When a facilitator structures the board well — with zones, prompts, and visual hierarchy — the discussion becomes spatial. Ideas cluster. Relationships emerge visually. Participants place their contributions physically next to related ideas.

**What makes it warm:** The physicality of placing a sticky note. The visual density of a well-used board communicates collective effort. Real-time cursors create co-presence. The infinite canvas means the conversation isn't constrained by a thread's linearity.

**Dezibel relevance:** LOW. Too corporate/workshoppy for Dezibel's aesthetic. But the principle of spatial discussion — where placement communicates meaning — is relevant.

## 3. Literary/Book Community Platforms That Feel Human

### Oku
**URL:** https://oku.club
**What it is:** A beautifully designed book tracking app. Member-supported (no ads, no Amazon affiliation). Founded as a direct response to Goodreads' stagnation.

**What it looks like:** Minimalist, clean, almost gallery-like. Book covers are displayed beautifully with generous spacing. The interface prioritizes the visual object of the book. Custom collections feel like curated shelves. The overall aesthetic is closer to a well-designed independent bookstore's website than a database.

**How it feels:** Like maintaining a personal library in a beautiful room. You track your reading, create custom collections, follow friends, and see what they're reading. The social layer is gentle — you discover books through people you trust, not algorithms or bestseller lists. The creators describe it as "the companion app for every book."

**What makes it warm vs cold:** Warm because the design respects the object (books look beautiful here). Warm because member-supported means "our interests are aligned with readers, not advertisers." Cold risk: it could feel lonely if your friends aren't on it.

**Small groups:** Following/friends model. Reading goals and stats. Import from Goodreads.

**Dezibel relevance:** HIGH. Oku proves that a book-adjacent community platform can survive and thrive on subscription revenue alone. Their design philosophy — beautiful, minimal, member-aligned — is exactly Dezibel's territory. The "discover through trusted people" model maps to cohort-based reading.

---

### Literal.club
**URL:** https://literal.club
**What it is:** A social book platform with tracking, reviews, highlights, and book clubs. Sleek, minimalist design. Stronger social layer than Oku.

**What it looks like:** Clean, modern interface. Book covers displayed prominently. Reviews use descriptive keyword tags (quick emotional reactions) alongside longer text reviews. Highlights can be saved and shared to social platforms. Club pages have moderated discussion spaces.

**How it feels:** Like the nicest book club you've ever been in. The keyword-tag review system is clever — you can leave a quick emotional impression ("atmospheric," "devastating," "slow burn") even if you don't have time for a full review. Book clubs have moderated membership (approve/reject), adjustable permissions, and structured discussion around highlights and reviews.

**What makes it warm:** Community-driven discovery — follow friends, see their highlights, discover books through personal recommendation rather than algorithm. The highlights feature turns reading into a sharable, social act. When a friend highlights a passage, you encounter their emotional reaction embedded in the text.

**Small groups:** Yes. Book clubs with moderated membership, shared reading lists, club-specific discussion. This is Literal's strongest differentiator.

**Dezibel relevance:** HIGH. The highlights-as-social-gesture concept is extremely relevant. If Dezibel readers could highlight moments in the story and share those highlights with their cohort, the discussion emerges naturally from the text rather than being imposed on top of it. The moderated book club model also maps to cohorts.

---

### The StoryGraph
**URL:** https://www.thestorygraph.com
**What it is:** A book tracking and recommendation platform founded by Nadia Odunayo. The main Goodreads alternative. Privacy-first. Content warnings. Mood-based recommendations.

**What it looks like:** Clean, dashboard-oriented. Strong data visualization — reading stats displayed as beautiful charts and graphs showing pace, mood patterns, genre distribution. Not as visually luxurious as Oku, but more functional.

**How it feels:** Data-rich but human. The mood-based recommendation engine asks you how you want to feel, not just what genre you want. Content warning tags are community-contributed and genuinely useful — they reflect care for the reader's emotional experience.

**What makes it warm:** The buddy reads feature is the killer intimacy mechanic. Up to 15 people read the same book simultaneously. You leave comments tied to specific pages — but your buddies can only see your comment once they reach that page themselves. This means the conversation unfolds in sync with the reading experience. No spoilers, no jumping ahead. You're walking through the book together.

**Small groups:** Buddy reads are built for groups of 2-15. Private. Synced to reading progress. This is the most intimate book discussion mechanic any platform has built.

**Dezibel relevance:** VERY HIGH. The buddy read mechanic is almost exactly what Dezibel needs. Comments tied to specific moments in the story, revealed only when you reach that moment — this is the dream. It turns discussion from a post-hoc activity into a living companion to the reading experience. StoryGraph proves this mechanic works and that readers love it.

---

### Marginalia
**URL:** https://getmarginalia.com
**What it is:** A social EPUB reader built around highlighting and annotation. Upload your own DRM-free books. See what the people you follow are highlighting in the same books.

**What it looks like:** A clean, distraction-free reading interface. When you select a passage, it's saved with page location, color choice, and optional notes. Other readers' highlights appear as soft layers in the text — you can see where others paused, what moved them.

**How it feels:** Like reading a used book full of someone else's underlines and marginalia — but from people you chose to follow. The annotations create a ghostly conversation in the text itself. No walled garden, no store, no lock-in — export everything as Markdown or JSON.

**What makes it warm:** Reading becomes collaborative without being performative. You're not writing reviews; you're just highlighting what hits you. When someone you follow highlighted the same line you did, the recognition is silent and powerful. It's the digital equivalent of finding that someone underlined the same passage in a library book.

**Small groups:** Follow-based. Follow readers whose taste you trust; their public highlights appear in your feed and in the books you share.

**Dezibel relevance:** VERY HIGH. Marginalia is the most directly relevant model for Dezibel's community layer. The annotation-in-the-text approach transforms the story itself into the discussion space. No separate forum, no thread — the conversation happens in the margins of the work. For a serialized novel delivered intimately, this is the natural community format.

---

### Bookship
**URL:** https://bookshipapp.com
**What it is:** A social reading app built around group book clubs. Read together, annotate together, discuss together. Built-in eReader. Video chat. Polls for book selection. Calendar for meeting scheduling.

**What it looks like:** Functional rather than beautiful. The emphasis is on the reading experience and group interaction, not visual design. Built-in eReader allows sharing notes and comments inside the book. Virtual Highlighter lets you extract quotes from physical books (photograph and OCR) to share.

**How it feels:** Like a digital book club infrastructure. Less about aesthetic beauty, more about removing friction from group reading. The "Cafe" is a public space for meeting other readers and joining public book reads.

**What makes it warm:** Spoiler tags on comments (hidden until you choose to reveal). Reading together in real-time through the shared eReader. Video chat embedded in the reading experience. Groups under 10 people are free.

**Small groups:** Core feature. Groups with TBRs (to-be-read lists), polls, calendars, reading progress tracking. Premium for groups over 10.

**Dezibel relevance:** MEDIUM. The group reading infrastructure is useful but the design is too utilitarian. The spoiler-tag mechanic is smart and directly applicable. The built-in eReader with shared annotations is relevant. But Bookship lacks the aesthetic soul that Dezibel needs.

## 4. Curated Discussion Spaces in Art/Culture

### The Creative Independent
**URL:** https://thecreativeindependent.com
**What it is:** A resource of emotional and practical guidance for creative people. Published by Kickstarter. One new interview every weekday since 2016. No ads, no PR pitches, no hype cycles.

**What it looks like:** Sparse, typographic, unhurried. Each interview begins with a short, warm bio (as if introducing the subject on stage), followed by the conversation, then a brief list of recommendations from the subject (ranging from a favorite movie to a preferred crisis hotline). The design is deliberately anti-magazine: no hero images, no pull quotes, no sidebar distractions.

**How it feels:** Like sitting in on a private conversation between two people who trust each other. The interviews are long, honest, and unstructured — they follow the subject's energy rather than an editorial agenda. Subjects range from Bjork to unknown ceramicists. The recommendation lists at the end feel like gifts — intimate glimpses into someone's daily life.

**What makes it warm:** The curation itself is the warmth. Someone chose this person. Someone asked these questions. Someone edited the transcript with care. The absence of advertising, metrics, and trending topics creates a space where honesty is possible. It's the anti-Twitter: slow, deep, one thing at a time.

**Small groups:** Not a group platform — it's one-to-many publishing. But the tone sets a template for how curated content can create the feeling of community without requiring interaction.

**Dezibel relevance:** HIGH. TCI demonstrates that curation can replace community features. A well-chosen interview, presented with care, creates a sense of belonging without requiring anyone to post. For Dezibel, this suggests that the story itself — delivered with the right pacing and framing — may be the primary community-building tool, not a discussion feature.

---

### Metalabel
**URL:** https://www.metalabel.com
**What it is:** A collective publishing platform. Groups of creatives form "labels" and release work together. Founded 2021-22 by seven collaborators worldwide. Supports art, design, games, music, performance, podcasts, publishing, video.

**What it looks like:** Clean, studio-quality design. Releases are "records of work" — digital packages with metadata, collaborator credits, and uploads (zip files, PDFs, video, audio). Labels can be solo artists, curators organizing others' releases, or groups working together.

**How it feels:** Like being part of a small record label, but for any creative medium. The structure makes collaboration visible and equitable — profit sharing is automatic, transparent, and agreed upon upfront. The emphasis on collective credibility over individual fame is the key design philosophy.

**What makes it warm:** The label metaphor creates belonging. You're not a solo creator shouting into the void; you're part of a named collective with a shared identity. Releases carry the label's name alongside individual credits. Revenue splits are built into the platform, removing the awkward money conversation.

**Small groups:** Core feature. Labels are inherently small groups (2-20 people typically). Shared publishing, shared audiences, shared finances.

**Dezibel relevance:** MEDIUM-HIGH. The label/collective model could inform how Dezibel structures its cohort experience. A cohort isn't just a subscription tier — it's a named group that experienced the story together. The "release" concept maps to serialized installments. The profit-sharing mechanics are less relevant but the identity layer is powerful.

---

### Substack Chat (When It Works)
**URL:** https://substack.com (Chat feature within newsletters)
**What it is:** A community chat feature within Substack newsletters. Writers create private discussion spaces for subscribers. Launched as experiment, now core feature.

**What it looks like:** Simple threaded chat within the Substack interface. Posts with images, links, text. Not visually distinctive — it looks like most messaging apps. The magic is in the context: it's attached to a specific writer's world.

**How it feels:** At its best, like a private group chat with the author and 50-300 superfans. Comics writers 3 Worlds / 3 Moons use it for viewing parties of classic films, early glimpses of new work, and AMAs. Some writers host weekly prompts ("what are you watching?"), book clubs (chapter-by-chapter discussion), or creative challenges.

**What makes it warm:** The intimacy comes from the small scale and the writer's presence. When an author with 10,000 subscribers has a chat room with 200 active members, those 200 feel chosen. The creator sets the tone. One-on-one DMs are also possible.

**What makes it cold:** When it's ignored by the creator, or when it scales past ~500 active users, the intimacy breaks. The UI is generic. It only works when the human behind it puts in the work.

**Dezibel relevance:** MEDIUM. Proves that author-adjacent community works at small scale. The limitations are instructive: Dezibel needs better UI, better integration with the reading experience, and structural intimacy (cohorts) rather than relying solely on the author's presence.

---

### Urbit Groups
**URL:** https://urbit.org
**What it is:** A decentralized personal server platform. Users form groups with shared channels (chat, notebooks, link collections). Everything is peer-to-peer, private, encrypted. Weird and intentional.

**What it looks like:** Landscape (the Urbit social layer) is minimal and functional. Groups have chat channels, notebook channels (long-form writing), and link collection channels. The aesthetic is deliberately un-flashy — it looks like a tool, not a product.

**How it feels:** Like joining a secret society that runs on its own internet. The barrier to entry is high (you need an Urbit ID, you run your own server), which creates a strong self-selection filter. The people who are on Urbit are there because they care deeply about owning their digital space. Conversations tend to be more thoughtful than on mainstream platforms because the friction filters for intention.

**What makes it warm:** The ownership model. Your data lives on your server. Your identity is yours. There's no company watching. This creates a different kind of trust — you're talking in a space that belongs to the participants, not a platform. The groups feel like private clubs in the best sense.

**Small groups:** Designed for small groups. Groups share channels. Everything is peer-to-peer. No central server means no central authority, no moderation by a company, no algorithmic interference.

**Dezibel relevance:** LOW for technology (too complex for mainstream adoption), HIGH for philosophy. Urbit proves that ownership and privacy create trust, and trust enables intimacy. The multi-channel group model (chat + notebooks + links) is a good template for cohort spaces.

## 5. Ephemeral/Time-Limited Art Experiences Online

### Rhizome / Net Art Anthology
**URL:** https://rhizome.org / https://anthology.rhizome.org
**What it is:** An American non-profit arts organization that champions born-digital art. Founded 1996 as an email list. In 2016, launched Net Art Anthology: a two-year online exhibition restaging 100 key works from net art history.

**What it looks like:** The archive is deliberately rough — it preserves the original look and feel of net art from the 1990s through the present, including broken links, outdated Flash, and obsolete browser formats. The Anthology is organized chronologically: early network cultures (pre-1998), Flash/blogs (1999-2005), surf clubs and social media (2006-2011), mobile apps (2012-present).

**How it feels:** Archaeological. Visiting Rhizome's archive feels like excavating digital history. The ephemeral nature of the work — artworks that "survive only as fragments and traces, offering glimpses of a larger networked context that can never be fully grasped" — creates a melancholic beauty. You know these works were alive once, responsive, connected. Now they're preserved but frozen.

**What makes it warm:** The care of preservation. Someone decided these ephemeral things mattered enough to save. Rhizome's digital preservation program creates open-source tools to decentralize web archiving, ensuring "continuous access to its collections of born-digital art." The warmth is curatorial love.

**Dezibel relevance:** MEDIUM. The ephemeral-but-preserved tension is deeply relevant to Dezibel. A serialized novel delivered over iMessage is inherently ephemeral — the experience of receiving it in real-time can never be replicated. But the traces can be preserved. Rhizome models how to honor the time-bound nature of an experience while still creating an archive.

---

### Do Not Research
**URL:** https://donotresearch.substack.com
**What it is:** A publishing platform for writing, visual art, and internet culture. Founded by Joshua Citarella, artist and internet-culture researcher. Operates as both a publication and community.

**What it looks like:** Hosted on Substack. Text-forward. The aesthetic is deliberately internet-native: essays about memetic subcultures, radical online politics, digital art. Contributors include visual artists, educators, and cultural researchers.

**How it feels:** Like reading dispatches from the digital frontier. The platform occupies the space between academic criticism and internet shitposting — it takes internet culture seriously as a subject of study while participating in it. The community is small and highly engaged, drawn by Citarella's research into online subcultures.

**What makes it warm:** The specificity. DNR doesn't try to be for everyone. It's for people who think about internet culture the way others think about film or literature — as a medium with its own history, aesthetics, and politics. This narrowness creates belonging.

**Dezibel relevance:** LOW-MEDIUM. The specificity-creates-community principle is relevant. DNR also demonstrates that Substack-as-platform works for niche cultural communities when the editorial voice is strong enough.

---

### Secret Cinema (COVID Digital Pivot)
**URL:** https://www.secretcinema.com
**What it is:** UK-based immersive cinema company. During COVID, pivoted to digital experiences: Zoom parties, virtual screenings with binaural sound and face filters, and eventually "virtual live" interactive shows with live actor interactions.

**What they did online:** Their first Zoom party was an 80s-themed event with DJs, selling 1,000+ tickets at 5 pounds each. They then developed full virtual productions: "Ghostbusters: The Gates of Gozer" was a 75-minute interactive show where online audience members engaged with live cast members through voice modulators, face filters, and binaural sound.

**How it felt:** Reports suggest the virtual events captured about 60% of the magic of the live shows. The key was live actors — real humans responding to the audience in real time, not pre-recorded content. Binaural sound created spatial immersion through headphones. Face filters and voice modulators gave participants permission to "become" characters, lowering inhibition.

**What makes it warm:** Human presence. Real actors reacting to real participants. The technology served the human connection, not the other way around. The time-gating (a specific show at a specific time) created the same "you had to be there" energy as live events.

**Dezibel relevance:** MEDIUM. The time-gating principle is directly applicable — Dezibel's daily delivery schedule already creates "you had to be there" energy. The live-actor interaction is less applicable (Dezibel is text-based), but the principle that human presence creates warmth is foundational.

---

### Punchdrunk / Sleep No More (Digital Extension)
**URL:** https://www.punchdrunk.com
**What it is:** The immersive theater company behind Sleep No More. With MIT Media Lab, they created an online companion world where remote participants partner with live audience members to explore the show together.

**How the digital extension works:** Online participants are paired with live audience members inside the McKittrick Hotel. The system delivers personalized multimedia content in real time, co-created by both the online participant's actions and their onsite partner's movements through the 100+ room, seven-story set. Each online participant has a unique experience.

**What makes it warm:** The pairing of a remote person with a live person. You're not watching a stream — you're tethered to a real human who is physically in the space. Their movements determine your experience. This creates an invisible intimacy between strangers.

**Dezibel relevance:** MEDIUM-HIGH. The concept of pairing remote participants — one person's experience shaping another's — is interesting for cohort design. Imagine two readers in the same cohort, reading at different paces, where the slower reader occasionally receives the faster reader's reactions as they catch up.

### e-flux
**URL:** https://www.e-flux.com
**What it is:** A publishing platform, curatorial project, and cultural infrastructure. Founded 1998. Publishes e-flux journal (academic art criticism), runs e-flux conversations (discussion platform), and creates experimental online projects.

**Notable projects:**
- **Time/Bank:** A platform where groups traded time and skills, bypassing money as value measurement.
- **e-flux video rental:** A free video rental shop for art video, proposing alternative distribution.
- **Institute of the Cosmos:** An ongoing collective research project investigating the materiality of the cosmos from philosophy, anthropology, science, and art perspectives.

**How it feels:** Intellectually rigorous but alive. e-flux occupies the intersection of art criticism, exhibition-making, and publishing in a way that feels more like a cultural institution than a platform. "e-flux conversations" (2014) was explicitly designed for "in-depth discussions of artistic and social ideas" — not comments sections, but structured dialogue.

**Dezibel relevance:** LOW-MEDIUM. e-flux's model of "platform as cultural institution" is aspirational but operates at a very different scale and audience than Dezibel. The Time/Bank project is conceptually interesting — what if Dezibel community currency was something other than money?

## 6. Discussion-as-Art Projects

### Miranda July's "Somebody"
**URL:** https://mirandajuly.com/somebody-app/ (app no longer active)
**What it is:** A messaging app (2014) where your message doesn't go to the recipient — it goes to the nearest Somebody user, who delivers it to your friend in person, verbally, acting as your stand-in. Launched at Venice Film Festival with Miu Miu.

**How it worked:** You type a message to a friend. The app finds the Somebody user geographically closest to your friend. That stranger receives your message and instructions (tone, gestures). They approach your friend and deliver the message as a mini-performance. The app included "hotspots" at museums (LACMA, New Museum, YBCA, Portland ICA, MFA Boston).

**How it felt:** Terrifying, tender, and electric. The app forced strangers into intimate encounters. July described undelivered messages as "floating" rather than failed — a poetic frame that treated the system's unreliability as a feature. When it worked, a stranger would approach you and say something deeply personal that your friend had written. The intimacy came from the collision of digital convenience and physical vulnerability.

**What makes it warm:** Everything. The entire premise is that human presence is more valuable than digital efficiency. The inefficiency IS the art. The risk of failure IS the intimacy. July called it "a far-reaching public art project, inciting performance and conversation about the value of inefficiency and risk."

**What makes it cold:** It barely worked. Messages often went undelivered. The app functioned "best as a participatory project rather than a reconceptualized form of social media." It was art, not infrastructure.

**Dezibel relevance:** VERY HIGH (conceptually). Somebody is the philosophical ancestor of what Dezibel could be. The core insight: inefficiency creates intimacy. The fact that delivery is uncertain, that a real person is involved, that timing matters — these "flaws" are what make it beautiful. Dezibel's iMessage delivery channel already has this quality: messages arrive in your actual life, at unpredictable moments, from a sender you have a relationship with.

### PostSecret
**URL:** https://postsecret.com
**What it is:** A community mail art project started by Frank Warren in 2004. People mail anonymous secrets on homemade postcards. Every Sunday, new postcards are uploaded to a simple blog. 500,000+ postcards received. Four million monthly visitors. Five NYT bestsellers.

**What it looks like:** A black background with postcards emerging from darkness — Warren describes them as "faces illuminated around a campfire." Each postcard is a handmade art object: collaged, painted, scrawled, photographed. Some are beautiful. Some are ugly. All are real. The website is deliberately simple: no comments, no profiles, no interaction beyond viewing.

**How it feels:** Like entering a confessional booth. The anonymity creates permission. The handmade quality creates humanity. The weekly cadence creates ritual. The black background creates focus. You scroll through strangers' darkest, funniest, most devastating truths and feel — as Warren puts it — "alone, together."

**What makes it warm:** "Secrets are the currency of intimacy." The project works because it inverts the usual social media equation: instead of performing your best self, you reveal your hidden self. The handmade postcards ensure that each secret carries the physical trace of its creator — handwriting, collage, stains. The vulnerability is the warmth.

**Small groups:** None. It's one-to-many. But the experience of reading creates the feeling of membership in a secret society of honest people.

**Dezibel relevance:** VERY HIGH. PostSecret proves that anonymous/semi-anonymous revelation creates community. The weekly cadence (new secrets every Sunday) mirrors Dezibel's delivery rhythm. The handmade quality of the postcards parallels the intimacy of iMessage delivery. And the core mechanic — sharing something real about yourself to a community that can receive it without judgment — is what Dezibel's community space should enable.

### Yoko Ono's Wish Tree (Digital)
**URL:** https://wishtreeforyokoono.com
**What it is:** A digital extension of Ono's ongoing Wish Tree installation (started 1996). Physical installations invite visitors to write wishes on paper and tie them to a tree's branches. Over 2 million wishes collected worldwide. All wishes eventually sent to the Imagine Peace Tower in Reykjavik. The digital version launched in 2023 for Ono's 90th birthday, created by Sean Ono Lennon.

**How it feels:** Simple and sacred. You write a wish. It joins millions of other wishes. There's no discussion, no reaction, no algorithm. The wish exists. During COVID, the installation moved online so people could "keep expressing their hopes for the future despite ongoing restrictions."

**What makes it warm:** The accumulation. Each individual wish is tiny and private. The collective is massive and moving. The knowledge that your wish will be sent to Reykjavik — physically transported to a monument — gives the digital act a weight that most online interactions lack.

**Dezibel relevance:** MEDIUM. The "small private gesture joining a massive collective" mechanic is interesting. Imagine Dezibel readers leaving a one-sentence reaction to each day's delivery — not a review, not a discussion post, just a wish/feeling/fragment — that accumulates into a collective emotional record of the reading experience.

## 7. The Anti-BBS: Warm Technology Examples

### Glass
**URL:** https://glass.photo
**What it is:** A paid, ad-free photo sharing community for photographers. No likes (or private-only likes). No algorithm. No follower counts. Chronological feed. Full EXIF data. Subscription-supported. Independent, no VC funding.

**What it looks like:** Photos displayed edge-to-edge, full-bleed, on dark backgrounds. No UI chrome competing for attention. EXIF data (camera, lens, settings) displayed prominently below each photo. Comments are the primary interaction — no hearts, no retweet equivalent. Categories for discovery. The overall aesthetic is a high-end photography portfolio site.

**How it feels:** Like walking through a gallery where every piece has a placard explaining how it was made. The EXIF data transforms comments from "nice pic!" to "what made you choose this focal length?" Technical discussion emerges organically. The absence of follower counts and public metrics removes the performance anxiety of Instagram. You post for craft, not clout.

**What makes it warm:** The subscription model aligns incentives — Glass serves photographers, not advertisers. The chronological feed means every post gets equal visibility. The EXIF emphasis signals "we care about how you made this" rather than "we care about how many people liked it." Comments are substantive because there's nothing else to do.

**Small groups:** Not explicitly — it's a single community. But the paid barrier (~$5/month) and niche focus (photographers only) create a naturally small, committed group.

**Dezibel relevance:** HIGH. Glass is the clearest proof that a paid, ad-free, metric-free community platform can work. The design principles map directly: respect the primary object (photos / story), remove vanity metrics, enable substantive discussion, align economic incentives with users. Glass is what "anti-Instagram" looks like for photography. Dezibel needs the equivalent for serialized fiction.

---

### VSCO
**URL:** https://www.vsco.co
**What it is:** A photo editing and creative community platform. No public likes. No follower counts. Subscription model. Known for aesthetic quality and creative seriousness.

**What it looks like:** Clean, photography-first interface. The editing tools are professional-grade. The community feed is chronological. VSCO Spaces are shared galleries organized around themes, styles, events, or locations. VSCO Canvas lets you build visual boards from community images.

**How it feels:** Like the photography internet before Instagram ruined it. The absence of likes and follower counts creates a different emotional register — people share work they're proud of, not work they think will perform. Community photowalks (Miami, San Francisco, Brooklyn) bring online connection into physical space.

**What makes it warm:** "A creative community free of likes and follower counts." The photowalks are brilliant — they translate digital community into physical encounter. VSCO Spaces allow collaborative curation around themes, creating micro-communities within the larger platform.

**Dezibel relevance:** MEDIUM. VSCO validates the no-metrics model at scale (millions of users). The Spaces concept (themed collaborative galleries) could translate to themed discussion spaces within Dezibel cohorts. The photowalks suggest that online communities become strongest when they occasionally break into the physical world.

### Loom (for Intimate Groups)
**URL:** https://www.loom.com (now Atlassian Loom)
**What it is:** Async video messaging. Record your screen and face, share the video. Recipients watch on their own time and respond with comments, emoji reactions, or their own video replies. Now owned by Atlassian.

**What it looks like:** Your face in a floating circle over your screen content. Videos are brief (typically 1-5 minutes). Auto-generated transcripts. Time-stamped comments along the video timeline. Emoji reactions at specific moments.

**How it feels:** More personal than email, less intrusive than a meeting. Seeing someone's face and hearing their voice while they walk you through something creates intimacy that text can't match. The async nature means no scheduling, no small talk, no "can you hear me?"

**What makes it warm:** The face. Video messages capture tone, body language, facial expressions — the human bandwidth that text strips out. Time-stamped comments create a conversation layer on top of the video, similar to annotation on a text.

**Dezibel relevance:** LOW-MEDIUM. Loom itself isn't relevant, but the principle of "async video as intimate communication" could inform Dezibel if the author wanted to occasionally send video messages to cohorts — a face-to-camera moment that readers watch on their own time.

---

### Private Podcast Feeds as Community
**Platforms:** Transistor, Hello Audio, Captivate, Patreon
**What it is:** Private RSS feeds distributed only to specific groups. Subscribers receive episodes in their regular podcast app but the feed isn't publicly listed. Used for membership communities, company communications, and intimate creator-subscriber relationships.

**How it feels:** Like receiving a personal letter in audio form. The podcast app is already an intimate space — you listen with headphones, often alone, often while doing something physical (walking, cooking, commuting). A private feed adds exclusivity: this is for you and your small group.

**What makes it warm:** The podcast medium itself is inherently intimate — a voice in your ear. Private feeds add the feeling of being chosen, of being inside the circle. The friction of subscription (RSS, login) creates a membership boundary.

**Dezibel relevance:** MEDIUM-HIGH. Private audio feeds could be a companion channel for Dezibel. Imagine the story arriving via iMessage, but the author's commentary/reflection arriving as a private podcast episode each week. Different medium, different register, same intimate relationship. The RSS model also means no platform dependency.

### Racket (Original Social Audio, now pivoted)
**URL:** racket.app (pivoted; original social audio platform no longer active)
**What it was:** A social audio app for 99-second micro-podcasts. TikTok-style vertical feed of audio snippets. Record from your phone browser, no editing needed. "Volleys" — threaded audio responses to other people's recordings.

**How it felt:** More intimate than tweets, less performative than podcasts. 99 seconds is long enough to express a real thought but short enough that you don't overthink it. The audio format captured tone, hesitation, emotion — the things text strips out. "Racket conversations feel so much more intimate than Tweets."

**What made it warm:** The constraint. 99 seconds forces authenticity — you can't polish, you can't strategize, you just talk. The "Volley" mechanic (responding with your own 99-second audio) created genuine back-and-forth conversation with the texture of voice.

**Why it died:** Pivoted to podcast investment. The social audio window (Clubhouse, Twitter Spaces) closed as pandemic isolation ended.

**Dezibel relevance:** MEDIUM. The 99-second constraint mechanic is interesting — what if Dezibel's community space had a "voice note" feature where readers could leave 60-second audio reactions to the day's delivery? Audio captures emotional response in a way text can't. The constraint prevents overthinking.

## Cross-Cutting Principles for Dezibel

### What makes online spaces feel warm:

1. **Absence of metrics.** No public likes, no follower counts, no algorithmic amplification. Every platform that feels warm has deliberately removed the scoreboard. (Glass, VSCO, Are.na, Cosmos, Oku)

2. **The primary object is respected.** The photo is shown full-bleed. The book cover is displayed beautifully. The art is experienced in its original format. The design says "this thing matters" before it says anything about the community. (Glass, Oku, New Art City, Marginalia)

3. **Curation replaces algorithm.** Discovery happens through human trust networks, not recommendation engines. You find things because someone you respect collected them, not because they went viral. (Are.na, Literal.club, The Creative Independent)

4. **Small scale is a feature.** StoryGraph caps buddy reads at 15 people. Bookship's free tier caps at 10. Substack Chat works at 200-300, breaks at 500+. Intimacy requires a boundary.

5. **Discussion emerges from the work, not alongside it.** Marginalia's annotations are IN the text. StoryGraph's buddy read comments are tied to specific pages. Figma's cursors are ON the canvas. The best discussion spaces aren't separate from the experience — they're woven into it.

6. **Presence over performance.** Seeing that someone else is reading right now (StoryGraph buddy reads), editing right now (Kinopio live spaces), or visiting right now (New Art City copresence) creates belonging without requiring anyone to post.

7. **Time creates scarcity creates value.** PostSecret publishes once a week. The Creative Independent publishes once a day. Dezibel delivers once a day. Ephemeral experiences (Secret Cinema, Somebody) create "you had to be there" energy. The constraint of time is what makes the experience feel precious rather than disposable.

8. **Inefficiency is intimacy.** Miranda July's Somebody app. PostSecret's handmade postcards. Urbit's self-hosted servers. When something is harder than it needs to be, the extra effort communicates care.

9. **Anonymity enables vulnerability.** PostSecret. The confession format. The permission to be honest comes from the protection of anonymity. For Dezibel, this might mean that community reactions can be anonymous — you know someone in your cohort felt devastated by today's delivery, but you don't know who.

10. **Paid access aligns incentives and creates commitment.** Glass, Oku, VSCO, Are.na. When users pay, the platform serves them. When they don't, it serves advertisers. The paywall also functions as a commitment device — people who pay show up differently.

## Specific Dezibel Community Space Recommendations

Based on this research, the Dezibel community space should draw from:

- **Marginalia's model:** Reactions embedded in the story itself, not in a separate forum
- **StoryGraph's buddy reads:** Comments revealed only when you reach that moment in the story
- **Are.na's curation-as-conversation:** Collecting and connecting moments rather than writing reviews
- **Glass's economics:** Paid, ad-free, metric-free
- **PostSecret's anonymity:** Permission for vulnerability through optional anonymity
- **New Art City's copresence:** Knowing others are experiencing the same thing right now
- **Kinopio's spatial thinking:** Non-linear, visual ways to respond to the story
- **The Creative Independent's editorial warmth:** Curation that creates belonging without requiring participation
- **Miranda July's philosophy:** Inefficiency and risk as features, not bugs
